Direct & Inverse Variation

What is Variation?

Variation describes how two quantities relate to each other as they change. There are two fundamental types: direct variation, where quantities grow together, and inverse variation, where one grows as the other shrinks.

Direct Variation

Two quantities x and y vary directly if increasing x causes y to increase proportionally. Their ratio always stays constant.

Direct Variation: y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality (k ≠ 0).

This means y/x = k always. If you double x, y doubles. If you halve x, y halves.

Key Property:

y1/x1 = y2/x2 = k

Example: Speed and Distance

A car travelling at constant speed covers 120 km in 2 hours. How far does it travel in 5 hours?

k = 120/2 = 60 km/h

Distance = 60 × 5 = 300 km

Inverse Variation

Two quantities x and y vary inversely if increasing x causes y to decrease such that their product stays constant.

Inverse Variation: y = k/x, or equivalently xy = k, where k is the constant of proportionality.

If you double x, y is halved. If you triple x, y becomes one-third.

Key Property:

x1y1 = x2y2 = k

Example: Workers and Time

8 workers can complete a job in 6 days. How long would 12 workers take?

k = 8 × 6 = 48

Time = 48/12 = 4 days

How to Identify Which Type

Quick Test

  • If y/x = constant → Direct variation
  • If x × y = constant → Inverse variation
  • Plot the points: a straight line through origin = direct; a curve (hyperbola) = inverse

Combined Variation

Sometimes a quantity varies with more than one other quantity at the same time.

Joint Variation: z varies directly with both x and y → z = kxy

Combined Variation: z varies directly with x and inversely with y → z = kx/y

Example: Combined Variation

The pressure P of a gas varies directly with temperature T and inversely with volume V. If P = 200 when T = 300 and V = 6, find k. Then find P when T = 360 and V = 4.

200 = k × 300/6 → k = 200 × 6/300 = 4

P = 4 × 360/4 = 360

Real-World Examples:

  • Direct: Cost of fuel vs. litres purchased
  • Inverse: Speed vs. time for a fixed journey
  • Combined: Force = mass × acceleration (joint variation)
